A Simple Sketch Effect

SEPTEMBER 17, 2019 | C++, OPENGL, GRAPHICS

I had always wanted to make 3D renderings appear hand-made such that they would look similar to traditional 2D animation. I was finally able to devise a technique that would create the effect I desired using tessellation and geometry shaders and a lot of fiddling about. It's not quite realistic but it still evokes the feeling I was going for.

Here it is used in a later project:

While this technique may look familiar, it is not derived from a basic cel shader. I wanted control over imperfections seen in traditional animations like imperfect shapes and lines being over-drawn or off-angle. Such effects cannot be easily achieved with a cel shader that has only depth and model-distinctions to work from. What I ended up developing is much more involved but gives far more variance to the end result.

1. Creating A Box

I started off with a box since it is enough demonstrate how various situations are handled. The technique works initially by drawing simple lines and builds from there. I'll go ahead and note that a purely wireframe model is not sufficient for the whole effect, the triangulated faces are used for multiple things in fact. More on that later.

2. Adding Randomness

As a first step, I adjusted the vertex shader to add some randomness the vertex's position. Its pretty subtle since I don't want the lines to be too sporadic, but there's already a bit of underlying complexity.

I made sure that the randomness is deterministic and based on both the vertex and a independent "frame" value that I only update 12 times a second. Its not very apparent on the 20fps gif above, but I assure you it is there. I had built this at 144fps and if I hadn't added the limit, it'd be nothing but a jittery mess.

3. Splitting Lines

Then I started treading in unfamiliar territory. I wanted to divide lines into segments so that I add more variation and not rely on just the vertex endpoints. I could have just remade the cube with intermediate points, but that adds a burden to the model creation and isn't adaptive to how its actually rendered.

This is done with a tessellation shader. If you don't know, a tessellation is an OpenGL feature where a control shader dictates how a primitive will be subdivided and an evaluation shader that dictates how the resultant points are generated. Here, it was pretty easy to take a line and create smaller lines. The trick was to make the tessellation dependent on screen space; you may be able to notice that the lines that are visibly shorter have less segments. That way, lines further away will stay a single line, and those closer to the screen get the additional detail they need.

As a side note, I made sure that the individual segments stay connected in this example, but they don't have to be.

4. Thickening Lines

OpenGL lines are very limited; they're only good for wireframes since you can't control them much beyond glLineWidth() and glLineStipple(). So I used a geometry shader to transform those lines into a triangles so that there is width that can be manipulated. At this point, they are just rectangles, but I do a bit more with them...

5. Adding Endcaps

Since stopping each line abruptly doesn't look that good, I extended the geometry shader to output eight vertexes per line instead of four. That way I could add some smaller bits on the ends to make them appear rounded. It doesn't look good at larger scales, but it looks convincing at a more modest line width.

Another side note, this was where I really started caring about it rendering correctly regardless of aspect ratio. Since the points are already in clip-space, extending from the points uniformly would appear stretched on most displays. I was really frustrated at first trying to account for the aspect ratio in all the required places, but I eventually just transformed the points similar to screen-space, did what I had to, and transformed them back to create the points. It was much simpler that way.

6. Using Depth

This is getting more complicated again; I don't want to draw lines that are obscured by being on the backside or even behind other objects. For that, I need depth.

So here's where I need the original traingular mesh from the model. I render the model to a separate framebuffer without any effects, textures, or even color since all I need is the depth image. I then send that to the geometry shader, which does the depth check manually, and can choose to omit that line. A lot of work, but now rendered objects look solid.
